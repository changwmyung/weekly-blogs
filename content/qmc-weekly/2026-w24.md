_A quiet but unusually conceptual week for neural-VMC: a serious attempt to import deep-learning **scaling laws** into NQS benchmarking, a **convolutional transformer** ansatz that finally seems to use the transformer machinery properly, and a fermionic NQS application to superconducting quantum-dot clusters. No new FermiNet variants, no fresh Hubbard energy records — just three papers that each push on a different axis (theory, architecture, application)._

## Highlights

- **Scaling laws hit NQS.** Rende et al. show the $V$-score of transformer wave functions on the $J_1$–$J_2$ Heisenberg model decays as a power law in training compute, with a frustration-dependent exponent. A clean way to compare ansätze that isn't "look at my mHa."
- **Convolutional transformer wave functions.** Chen, Naik, Heyl argue earlier transformer NQS were undertuned and propose CTWF, claiming SOTA on both ground-state search and nonequilibrium dynamics.
- **Fermionic NQS for superconducting QD clusters.** Žonda et al. canonical-transform their coupled-dot model into a particle-conserving form so they can throw standard fermionic NQS at it, and benchmark against DMRG.

## Papers

### Scaling Laws for Neural-Network Quantum States
_Rende, Sinibaldi, Viteritti, Wiersema, Georges, Carleo_ · [arXiv:2606.02794](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02794v1) · cond-mat.dis-nn

Treats the $V$-score of a transformer ansatz on $J_1$–$J_2$ Heisenberg (triangular and square, up to $20\times 20$) as the analog of an LLM loss curve, and finds it decays as a power law in training compute. Under an appropriate compute rescaling, curves for different $L$ collapse onto a single master curve, and the exponent is approximately $L$-independent — i.e. the transformer ansatz is **size-consistent** for these models. The exponent itself decreases with frustration $J_2/J_1$, giving a quantitative knob for "how hard is this ground state to represent." This is the most useful methodological paper of the week: it gives the community a benchmarking protocol that doesn't reduce to a single-number energy contest.

### Convolutional transformer wave functions
_Chen, Naik, Heyl_ · [Phys. Rev. Research](https://doi.org/10.1103/7xwp-25y9) · PRResearch

Argues that the transformer NQS literature has been leaving performance on the table by not properly combining attention with the locality priors that make CNNs good for lattice models. Their CTWF interleaves convolutional and attention layers and reportedly beats prior transformer wave functions on **both** ground-state search and real-time dynamics — the latter is the harder claim and worth scrutinizing once a code drop appears. The paper is short on architectural ablation, so it's not yet clear which ingredient (conv stem, attention pattern, parameterization) does the heavy lifting; pair this with the Rende et al. scaling-law framework above and you'd actually have an apples-to-apples comparison.

### Correlated States in Quantum Dot Clusters Coupled to a Common Superconductor
_Žonda, Rękas, Poláček, Kodrlová, Pokorný, Friák_ · [arXiv:2606.04608](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04608v1) · cond-mat.str-el

The clever step is methodological: a canonical transformation maps the proximity-coupled dot cluster onto a particle-number-conserving Hamiltonian, which removes the main reason fermionic NQS struggle with superconductors (BCS-style states are not number eigenstates). After that they run **standard** fermionic NQS alongside DMRG and exact diagonalization, and map out three regimes — trivial SC singlet, Heisenberg-like strongly correlated, and a gapless critical intermediate phase in 1D with singlet-doublet sequences; 2D clusters show robust triplet ground states. The headline for NQS practitioners is that "off-the-shelf" architectures work once the representation is fixed, which is a more interesting claim than "we built a new ansatz."

## Trends & Themes

The unifying thread this week is **stop arguing about mHa and start measuring representational difficulty**: Rende et al. propose scaling exponents, Chen et al. force a head-to-head against prior transformer wave functions on two tasks (statics and dynamics), and Žonda et al. show that with the right basis even a standard fermionic NQS handles a non-trivial superconducting model. If you've been on the fence about whether transformer-style architectures are actually doing something nontrivial for lattice ground states, this is a good week to revisit that prior.
